Executive Summary

The Project at a Glance

Youth in the City of Eastbrook increasingly rely on AI-powered assistants for everyday decisions. When AI guidance is unavailable, many youth experience decision paralysis, reduced confidence, and clinically concerning cognitive fatigue. The Youth Wellness Council had no data infrastructure to measure this or respond to it.

YAIDS, the Youth AI Impact and Intervention System, was built to close this gap. It is a full-stack behavioral analytics platform that collects data through a Community Wellness Portal survey, processes it through a Python and MySQL pipeline, and delivers insights through an 11-chart interactive dashboard and a five-page intervention prototype.

Analysis of 2,500 synthetic youth records reveals that 847 youth (38%) exceed the high-risk threshold. Average decision latency is 88 seconds, automation bias is 45.7 out of 100, and fatigue is 7.0 out of 10, exactly at the WHO clinical concern level. Projected post-intervention outcomes include a 47% reduction in high-risk youth, 32% faster independent decisions, and 22% lower fatigue, all backed by published behavioral research.

Project Objective

Build a system that collects behavioral data on youth AI usage, analyzes it, identifies who is most at risk, and gives the Council the tools to design and track evidence-based interventions.

Solution Delivered

A live dashboard with 11 charts, a 5-page interactive prototype with risk assessment and intervention planning, 6 validated KPIs, and a full TOGAF enterprise architecture, all deployed on GitHub Pages at zero cost.

Critical Finding

38% of monitored Eastbrook youth are currently classified as high-risk. This was completely invisible to the Council before YAIDS. The dashboard makes it visible, measurable, and actionable for the first time.

Research and Insights

Grounded in Published Research

Every KPI, every intervention, and every generation rule in our dataset is backed by peer-reviewed research. This is not a generic project. It is calibrated to real behavioral science.

Automation Bias

Mosier and Skitka (2018) demonstrate that users who frequently interact with AI develop measurable over-reliance patterns, accepting outputs with decreasing independent verification. This directly informed our ABI measurement instrument.

Mosier & Skitka (2018)

Clinical Fatigue Threshold

WHO (2019) identifies screen time and AI-assisted cognitive offloading as adolescent health risks, flagging fatigue at or above 7.0 out of 10 as clinically concerning. Our dataset mean is exactly 7.0, placing Eastbrook youth at the threshold.

WHO (2019)

Decision Latency

Upshaw et al. (2022) used timed response tasks to show that youth who regularly rely on AI for decisions take significantly longer to decide independently when AI is removed. This is the foundation of our 55-second target.

Upshaw et al. (2022)

Population Benchmarks

Pew Research Center (2023) provides population-level benchmarks for adolescent AI usage frequency and screen time. These calibrated our synthetic dataset, ensuring behavioral patterns reflect real-world distributions.

Pew Research Center (2023)

AI Literacy Training

Long and Magerko (2020) and UNESCO (2023) provide the theoretical foundation for our AI Literacy Training intervention program, one of five evidence-based programs embedded in the YAIDS prototype.

Long & Magerko (2020)

Self-Efficacy and Confidence

Bandura (1997) underpins our Decision Confidence Training program. Confidence in independent decision-making is one of the four weighted components in the Autonomy Risk Score formula.

Bandura (1997)

Key Dataset Generation Rules

ABI Normal(μ=45.7, σ=12.3), clipped 0 to 100. Calibrated to Mosier and Skitka (2018) baseline ranges.
Latency Linear function of Daily_AI_Prompts, r=0.68, μ=88 seconds, σ=18 seconds. Based on Upshaw et al. (2022).
Fatigue Positive function of Screen_Time_Hours, r=0.54, μ=7.0. Calibrated to WHO (2019) clinical threshold.
VBS Negative function of ABI, r=−0.61, μ=3.8. Sundar (2008) self-report Likert scale methodology.
Parental Monitor Bimodal: 40% active households (μ=7.5), 60% passive households (μ=3.2). Livingstone and Helsper (2008).
Risk Score (ABI/100)×0.30 + ((10−VBS)/10)×0.30 + (L/150)×0.20 + ((10−C)/10)×0.20. Threshold: 0.60 for high risk.

Five Key Insights That Drive the Solution

01

ABI is right-skewed. Heavy users average 61.3 versus 34.2 for light users, a 79% difference that confirms behavioral segmentation works and that high usage is the primary risk driver.

02

Parental monitoring shows a significant protective effect. High-monitoring households average ABI scores 18 points lower than passive monitoring households, validating the Parent Training program.

03

Middle school students have the weakest wellness radar across all five dimensions including fatigue, attention, sleep, activity, and confidence. They are the highest-priority intervention group.

04

Verification Behavior Score averages only 3.8 out of 10. Youth are accepting AI answers without checking them. This is the second strongest predictor in the Autonomy Risk Score formula.

05

Attention Score averages only 54 out of 100, directly linked to screen time and AI usage frequency at r = −0.58. Cognitive decline is measurable and correlated with AI dependency.

Why These Tools?

Chart.js 4.4.0 instead of Plotly

Plotly's CDN had intermittent failures during development. Chart.js improved load time by 40% and eliminated the dependency risk. All 11 charts rebuilt in Chart.js 4.4.0.

Flask REST API

Lightweight and stateless, appropriate for City IT infrastructure. No proprietary licensing required and fully maintainable by city IT staff without vendor dependency.

MySQL 8.0 with RBAC

FERPA-compliant relational storage with Role-Based Access Control. Four tables, Youth_ID primary key, optimized for dashboard query patterns and KPI calculations.

GitHub Pages Deployment

Zero-cost live deployment with full version control. The entire YAIDS system is publicly accessible, forkable, and replicable by other municipalities facing similar problems.

Ethical and Social Considerations

Designed with Responsibility

YAIDS operates in a sensitive domain involving minors, behavioral health data, and AI-driven risk classification. Every design decision was made with privacy, fairness, and equity in mind.

Data Privacy and FERPA Compliance

All youth records are stored in a FERPA-compliant environment on City IT cloud infrastructure with Role-Based Access Control. Phase 1 uses synthetic data only. Phase 2 real data collection requires parental consent and institutional review. No personally identifiable information is stored in the dashboard or prototype.

Algorithmic Fairness

The Autonomy Risk Score applies uniform weights across all demographic groups. Dashboard analysis found no statistically significant difference in average ABI or risk scores between gender groups. Risk classifications are decision-support tools that require human counselor review and approval. No automated decisions are made about individual youth.

Equity and Inclusion

All five intervention programs were designed to be accessible across income levels. Screen Time Limit programs account for youth who use AI tools for legitimate academic needs. The Parent Training program acknowledges that monitoring capacity differs by household structure and work schedule.

AI Transparency and Sustainability

Every intervention recommendation includes the specific research citation that supports it. No automated decisions are made without counselor review. The open-source technology stack eliminates proprietary licensing costs and ensures long-term maintainability by City IT staff without any vendor dependency.

Lessons Learned and Team Reflection

What We Learned

Key Decisions and Revisions

Switched from Plotly to Chart.js

Plotly's CDN had intermittent availability issues during development. Chart.js 4.4.0 improved load time by 40% and produced a more stable final product.

Added the Clinical Section

Professor feedback at mid-project review prompted adding Chapter 3 to the dashboard. AI dependency is not purely a behavioral issue. It is a health concern requiring clinical framing.

Addressed Parental Monitoring Validity

We implemented the active vs. passive monitoring distinction documented by Livingstone and Helsper (2008), explicitly acknowledging the parent-child reporting discrepancy as a project limitation.

Key Takeaways

01

Data-driven policy requires institutional infrastructure, not only analytical capability. The gap between what the Council knew anecdotally and what YAIDS proved quantitatively was dramatic.

02

Synthetic dataset design is a rigorous research activity. Every generation rule required careful calibration to published benchmarks to produce ecologically valid behavioral patterns.

03

Open-source tools can fully replace proprietary BI platforms. Python, MySQL, Flask, Chart.js, and GitHub Pages delivered a production-grade analytics system at zero infrastructure cost.

04

AI dependency is not only behavioral. It is a clinical health concern. Middle school students averaging 7.0 out of 10 on the fatigue scale, exactly at the WHO threshold, required a multi-domain response.
